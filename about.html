Our Trip & Program

Global Health Learning • Community Partnership • Sustainable Impact

Who We Are

In April 2026, a team of 17 medical students from Marshall University’s Joan C. Edwards School of Medicine will travel to rural northern Malawi for a 3.5-week service-learning experience. Led by the student leadership of our Global & Rural Medicine Interest Group, this program marks the first opportunity in several years for Marshall medical students to return to an international clinical rotation following pandemic-related pauses.

We are a diverse group with backgrounds in medicine, pharmacy, and public health. Multiple students have traveled to Malawi previously and are returning to complete their malaria research. We are faculty-supervised, community-engaged learners with a long-term commitment to underserved care.

Program Focus & Commitment

Ethical, Community-Guided Learning

The purpose of this trip is to engage in ethical, community-guided global health learning while supporting locally identified service projects in Malawi. As medical students, our focus is on learning from Malawian clinicians and communities, building cultural humility, and contributing in ways that are sustainable and respectful.

  • Learning from the Community: We learn under the supervision of Malawian clinicians who lead patient care and public health efforts.
  • Sustainable Contribution: Our goal is to contribute to locally identified service projects without imposing outside solutions.
  • Cultural Humility: We strive to build perspective that strengthens our care for underserved populations in West Virginia and beyond.

Student-Led Initiative

Our project is founded and managed by a dedicated group of students from the Marshall University School of Medicine. While we operate independently, we are committed to upholding the highest standards of professional and ethical medical engagement.

Meet the Team

Background & Context

Northern Malawi and the Ekwendeni community share striking parallels with rural Appalachia. Both face challenges with travel distances to care, limited healthcare resources, workforce shortages, and high burdens of preventable disease.

Malawi, known as the "Warm Heart of Africa," relies on a small number of hospitals and outreach programs. In Ekwendeni, preventable illnesses such as malaria, tuberculosis, HIV, and childhood malnutrition remain prevalent.

Global Medical Education Foundation

Our commitment is exemplified by the Global Med Ed nonprofit, which has directed $17,000 to the Ekwendeni Health Sciences College to invest in future Malawian healthcare professionals.

Strengthening Care in WV

Learning in this setting equips students with adaptability and problem-solving skills that directly strengthen their ability to serve West Virginia.
